iDénwurts

Verbs

Verbs in Lanwurq make heavy use of clitics and affixation, morphemes that attach onto the root in order to create meaning in the form of various tenses, aspects, moods and voices.

Infinitive

Lanwurq infinitives are formed with the prefix qe- (cognate to English "to"), such as in qescrív "to write" from the verb stem -scrív-, which will trigger lenition on the verb stem if applicable: -ge- "go" > qeghe "to go". The Lanwurq infinitive verb can inflect for several moods and voices such as qeescríviq "to have written" or qebhi scríviq "to be written", or to be cheeky and mix them both: qeebhín scríviq "to have been written". Further information about how infinitives inflect for each mood and voice will be given in the descriptions of the moods/voices below.
When the verb begins in j, the infinitive prefix becomes qi-: -jara "to earn" > qijara.

Affirmative vs Interrogative

Below you will see lots of references about "affirmative" and "interrogative". This is because, in Lanwurq, there are a set of clitics which serve to turn a sentence into a question. They can act as verbal clitics or nominal TAM. Since the clitics vary according to the tense and aspect, I'll list the interrogative forms for each tense/aspect/mood as they come up.

Person Marking

Lanwurq verbs agree to the person of both the subject and object. The subject is marked by a proclitic on the verb phrase while the object is marked with a suffix on the verb itself. While infinitive verbs can take on object marking suffixes,qefangiq "to seize it", they can't take on subjects, unless the subject follows the preposition fur ,or fur inflects to person if the subject is a pronominal: furmi qefauing "for me to seize", fur ihon qefauing "for the hand to seize". To mark the subject the, infinitive prefix qe- is removed and replaced with person marking clitics as will be shown below.
The person marking clitics come in two forms which are "unemphatic" and "emphatic". The emphatic serves to place extra emphasis on the person being marked. In English this is done by placing extra stress on a pronoun e.g "you ate my food!" vs "you ate my food!". Instead of this, Lanwurq uses a different set of markers where the vowels vary from the unemphatic. The unemphatic is simply the default form. Do note that not all persons have an emphatic form. Note that person marking clitics or suffixes always have a high tone no matter where they are.

If the verb stem ends in r then the suffixes -r becomes -ur: atsí "I would see" > atsír "I would see her" vs adleðurur "I would beat her".

Note that if the verb is accompanied by a preposition, and the object is indirect, then the object suffix will attach to the preposition, as prepositions also inflect for perosn. If the object is a direct object then it attaches directly to the verb.

  • Indirect object: qeghlýar iq "to stare at" > schighlýart iqm "she stared at him"
  • Direct: qebhng er "to toss over" > abhngiqiq er "I tossed it over"

Note that when the object is a noun, it is optional to mark the third person object suffixes on the verb. So "she tussled my hair" can be ihaaremi schimalagrúst or ihaaremi schimalagrústiq with no difference in meaning.

Alterations caused by person marking

Sometimes when a subject clitic or an object suffix is placed before or after a certain sound, it can cause some changes on the clitic/suffix or the verb stem itself:

  • If the verb stem begins in d then the 3S.neut clitic iq- will drop the glottal stop and lengthen the vowel allophonically. This is thanks to the sound change [Vʔd] → [Vːd].
    • iq- + dihtiqðm "rubbed them" > idihtiqðm "it rubbed them"
  • If a verb stem ends in a vowel followed by n then the n will drop and the vowel will become nasal when the object clitics -mi, -mí, -ðm are attached. The same rule applies on progressive endings when the ending is -in and the past tense suffix -n.
    • wudban "would would curse" > wudbañðm "we would curse them"
    • wurbanin "we curse" > wurbaniñðm "we curse them"
    • echon "he named" > echoñmi "he named me"
    • iévs aúqpún fe iwaqur "I pulled the small nets out of the water" > iévs aúqpúñðm fe iwaqur
  • f the verb ends in -o following a vowel or -oñ then then any object clitics that end in vowels, including -m, -r will cause the vowel to restore to an l. In the case of the -m suffix it is then subject to Low Central Vowel Dropping and so the sequence lum becomes ôm. When the suffix -r comes before this restored l it gains an epenthetic vowel to become -ur.
    • qechio "to kill" > qechilur "to kill her"
    • qechio "to kill" > qechiôm "to kill him"
    • qeeqoñ "to mean" > 'seqlin "it means"
  • When the verb begins in j, the proclitics je-, e-, þe- become ji-, i-, þi-.
    • qijara "to earn" > jijarad "you earned", ijarad "he earned", þijarad "they earned"

Progressive Formation

Before we move onto describing the actual TAM, it is important to list how the progressive is formed, as while it is called "progressive" it is used in other constructions, such as the imperfect present as well as actual progressive aspects.
The formation of the progressive may seem daunting at first, but every form is incredibly regular. Since the progressive is marked not only with suffixes but with verbal proclitics (if the subject was a dropped pronoun) or nominal TAM (if the subject is a noun) so first I will explain those before the verb endings.

If the subject is a noun then the enclitic -s-, or -is if the verb ends in s or sch, or -ís if the noun ends in e, is attached to the end of the noun phrase instead of on the verb itself. The same goes for the plural clitic -r-: iducs þiq ibhéns þetchyñðmr slíqm "the dogs that chewed the bones are sleeping". Here the enclitic -r attaches to the verb þetchyñðm "they chewed them" as that verb is at the end of the noun phrase even though the -r is marking the progressive for the verb slíqm "sleeping". To translate the sentence word for word: "the dogs that the bones they chewed=are sleeping". Here are more examples of the progressive clitics -s, -r on subject nouns rather than verbs.

  • þurfórlíqnmi "they foresake me" > maléarnursr fórlíqn "my teachers foresake me"
  • iphéphur ísfuñphliniq "he's crumpling the paper" > iphéphur Anggisis fuñphliniq "Angus is crumpling the paper"
  • 'sdýn "it's expiring" > ifleschis dýn "the meat is expiring"
  • schísdngenus "she is ignoring us" > urmiðurs dingenus "our mother is ignoring us"

Note that if the plural i- is used on a definite noun, then it will become ið-, e.g iducsr slíqm "the dogs are sleeping" vs iðiducs slíqm? "are the dogs sleeping?". This is because the definite article used to be þi but the dental fricative was lost leaving i. However when the plural interrogative clitic i'- was used it remained to prevent a vowel hiatus and it was reanalysed as being part of the interrogative clitic.

i- trí i- i- cóns sclijm -n -iq > itrí iðichóns sclijmniq?
the tree are the squirrels climb -ing it are the squirrels climbing the tree?

One exception to plural nouns being marked with i- is when the noun follows aphuchuo "a few, some". In this case the typically singular s- is used, e.g saphuchuo eiducs slíqm? "are some/a few dogs sleeping?". This only happens in the interrogative, the affirmative uses the plural, e.g aphuchuo eiducsr slíqm "a few dogs sleep".

The verbs qecheñ "to know" and qehé "to have" do not take progressive forms in the present but rather use subject proclitics with no progressive ending; acheñ "I know", with qecheñ taking the suffix -s in the third person singular; echeñs "he knows" and qehé becoming hus in the third person singular; schihus "she has".
In the interrogative they take the interrogative proclitic d- which becomes ts- in the third person singular: dacheñ? "do I know?", tsecheñ? "does she know?", tsschihéiq? "does she have it?". The negative of these verbs is formed with the particle deñ: adeñ ceñ "I don't know", adeñ hé "I don't have". The participle forms of these verbs are cenin and hén. These are used mostly in inflected participles such as cenina "that I know".

Imperfect Present

The imperfect present deals with present tense actions that occur in the present, in English this is shown by examples like "I walk" or "she walks". In Lanwurq this is formed by using a progressive construction as shown above with the addition of progressive subject clitics. Whereas in English such a construction would denote the present progressive, in Lanwurq there was a shift in tenses and the old present progressive came to be used for the imperfect present. However there are some verbs that form the imperfect present by using a typically gnomic construction. Those verbs are qecheñ, qesí, qehé as is shown above.

  • qehých "to dig, to pluck, to pick out from the ground"
    • ahéo amhyqngjn "I dig a hole"
    • ahéo mahyqngjn? "am I digging a hole"
    • ataqe schíshyqngjn "she picks a potato"
    • ataqe sschihyqngjn? "is she picking as potato"
  • qehnc "to think"
    • schíshiñqng aneñqm "she thinks about him"
    • sschihiñqng aneñqm? "is she thinking about him?"
  • qes'chans "to shine"
    • ilihts scañsn "the light shines"
    • siliht scañsn? "is the light shining?"
  • qebhigh "to build"
    • iwurcísr bighn "the labourers are building"
    • iðiwurcís bighn? "are the labourers building?"

The negative is formed by placing the particle nó before the verb, any subject clitics will attach to this. e.g isódjur amnó cionm "I'm not killing the soldier".
The interrogative negative is formed by the particle nó plus the interrogative progressive clitics placed before the subject clitics, e.g isódjur manó cionm? "Am I not killing the soldier?". An alternative is to attach the prefix ne- but this only works in the affirmative: isódjur amnechionm "I'm not killing the soldier".

When the object of a verb is not a specific entity but rather a broad grouping it can be placed in the plural with a possessive clitic attached when the verb is qelijch "to like", qeló "to love" or qewañq "to want". Less often the verb can be any verb normally associated with the object. E.g urbuics schíslijqngðm "she likes books" - literally "her books she likes", or urbuics schísrídnðm "she reads books" - literally "her books she reads". In sentences with two verbs in English such as "she likes to read", in Focurc the second verb can be expressed as a possessed gerund e.g urrídn schíslijqngiq "she likes to read" - literally "her reading she likes". Such a construction can also be formed by placing fur "for" before the verb: schíslijqng fur qeríd "she likes to read". It seems that the former construction isn't used if the object is human. So for example *urfóch schíslijqng "she likes people" is grammatical but not preferred, instead human objects are not possessed: ifóch schíslijqng "she likes people" is the preferred form. Also is the object is explicitly owned by someone else then it won't have possessive proclitics.

When the subject of an imperfect present verb is a noun, then the noun takes on a clitic rather than the verb taking on a person marking clitic. For singular and collective nouns this clitic is -s this causes the same devoicing and delenition as the plural suffix and for plural nouns it is -r. When interrogative singular/collective nouns take the proclitic s- and plural nouns take the proclitic i- which becomes ið- on definite nouns:

  • ibhódahs bóqn "the old man helps"
  • ibhódahsr bóqn "the old men help"
  • sibhódah bóqn? "does the old man help?"
  • ibhódahs bóqn? "do old men help?"
  • iðibhódahs bóqn? "do the old men help?"

Imperfect Near Future

The near future is used for events that are about to occur in the near future. It is important to know that there is no set distinction between the near and far future as context plays a heavy role. Typically if the action can be done right away after speaking, such as eating, walking, going to bed etc, then the near future implies that the action will be done as soon the speaker finishes talking. If the action is one that can't be done right away then the near future implies that the action will be done as soon as is feasible (this heavily depends on context). For example if someone says Efrich aqsmighoñqevégh teiq "I will travel to Africa (in the near future)" then it may imply that the person is travelling within the next few days or weeks. The speaker may insert an adverb which specifies a time, e.g iephoñs aqsmighoñqechófðm imóra "I will buy apples tomorrow".

The near future is formed by leaving the verb in the infinitive and by attaching the proclitic aqs-, which is formed from aq "that" and -s- "copula, is", after which an object suffix is attached depending on the person, after that the prefix g(h)oñ- is placed (whether it is lenitied or not depends on if the person marking suffix ends in a vowel or not). The interrogative is formed by placing the -s- clitic before aq creating saq-. The aqs- can be shortened to 's-: 'smighoñqe
